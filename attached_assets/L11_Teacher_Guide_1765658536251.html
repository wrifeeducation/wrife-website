üí° Big Idea Summary

Lesson 11 introduces the Subject + Verb + Object sentence pattern, building on the Noun + Verb foundation from Lesson 10. This is the first time pupils see the complete basic sentence structure. The pattern is heavily scaffolded with colour-coding (blue, red, purple) and clear definitions. Pupils learn that sentences have three main parts that work together: WHO/WHAT does something (subject), WHAT they do (verb), and WHAT receives the action (object).

Colour Code System

Blue = Subject (WHO or WHAT does the action)
Red = Verb (the ACTION word)
Purple = Object (RECEIVES the action)

‚ö†Ô∏è Essential Warnings

  • Heavy Scaffolding Required: L11 is the first time pupils see the full Subject-Verb-Object pattern. Spend adequate time modeling with multiple examples.
  • Colour-Coding is Critical: Use the colour system consistently throughout (blue=subject, red=verb, purple=object). This visual support is essential for understanding.
  • Not All Verbs Have Objects: Some pupils may notice sentences without objects. Acknowledge this briefly but focus on the SVO pattern for this lesson.

üìù PWP Formula Preparation

Today's Formula: Subject + Verb + Object

Example: "My dog chased the ball."

  • Subject (blue): My dog
  • Verb (red): chased
  • Object (purple): the ball

Pupils will write ONE sentence using this formula in their PWP Booklet, then identify if it's Beginning, Middle or End of their story.

üéØ Teaching Steps (Complete Script) ‚ñº

Step 1: Connection & Review (3 minutes)

Purpose: Connect to previous learning and activate prior knowledge of Noun + Verb pattern

SAY: "Last lesson, we learned about the Noun + Verb pattern. Can anyone remind me what a noun is? What about a verb? Excellent! Today we're going to learn about a complete sentence pattern that uses three parts. By the end of today, you'll be able to write sentences using this pattern in your PWP."

DO:

  • Display slide 1 (title slide)
  • Briefly review Noun + Verb with 1-2 examples
  • Generate enthusiasm: "This is one of the most important patterns in English!"

Step 2: Review Previous Formula (5 minutes)

Purpose: Bridge from Lesson 10 to today's expanded pattern

SAY: "Let's review what we learned in Lesson 10. We practiced the Noun + Verb pattern. For example: 'The dog barked.' What's the noun? What's the verb? Great! Today we're going to add ONE more part to make our sentences even better."

DO:

  • Write on board: Noun + Verb
  • Get 3-4 quick examples from pupils
  • Transition: "Now let's see what happens when we add another part..."

Step 3: Introduce Subject, Verb, Object (15 minutes)

Purpose: Explicitly teach the three parts with heavy scaffolding

Part A: Introduce SUBJECT (5 min)

SAY: "A SUBJECT is the WHO or the WHAT that does something in a sentence. It's the person, animal or thing doing the action. We're going to use the colour BLUE for subjects. Let me show you..."

DO:

  • Display slides 3-4 (What is a Subject? + Examples)
  • Write on board in BLUE marker: "Mr. Brown met us."
  • Circle/underline "Mr. Brown" in blue
  • Ask: "WHO met us? Mr. Brown! He's the subject."
  • Show 2-3 more examples from "A Visit to the Farm"

Part B: Introduce VERB (5 min)

SAY: "A VERB is the action word. It tells us what the subject DOES. We use the colour RED for verbs because red means action, like a stop sign or a red light. Watch..."

DO:

  • Display slides 5-6 (What is a Verb? + Examples)
  • Return to "Mr. Brown met us" on board
  • Circle/underline "met" in RED marker
  • Ask: "What did Mr. Brown DO? He met us!"
  • Show 2-3 more verb examples

Part C: Introduce OBJECT (5 min)

SAY: "An OBJECT is the part that RECEIVES the action. It answers the question 'what?' or 'who?' after the verb. We use PURPLE for objects. Let me show you how it works..."

DO:

  • Display slides 7-8 (What is an Object? + Examples)
  • Return to sentence: "Mr. Brown met us"
  • Circle/underline "us" in PURPLE marker
  • Ask: "Mr. Brown met WHO? Us! We're the object."
  • Show sentence pattern: Subject + Verb + Object
  • Display slide 9 (The Sentence Pattern)

üí° Teaching Tip

Use exaggerated gestures to reinforce each part. Point to yourself for SUBJECT, make action movements for VERB, and point away for OBJECT. This kinesthetic support helps memory.

Step 4: Whole Class Practice with Text (10 minutes)

Purpose: Guided practice identifying all three parts in context

SAY: "Now let's practice together with our story 'A Visit to the Farm.' I'll read it aloud, and then we'll find the subject, verb and object in some sentences. Remember: blue for subject, red for verb, purple for object!"

DO:

  • Display story text on board/screen
  • Read "A Visit to the Farm" aloud with expression
  • Display slides 12-14 (Practice slides)
  • Work through 3-4 sentences together:
    • "The cows were eating hay" ‚Üí Subject? Verb? Object?
    • "We visited the barn" ‚Üí Subject? Verb? Object?
    • "The chickens were laying eggs" ‚Üí Subject? Verb? Object?
  • Use Think-Pair-Share: think individually, discuss with partner, share with class

üí° Questioning Strategy

Always ask in this order:

  1. "What is the ACTION word?" (verb - easiest to spot)
  2. "WHO or WHAT does that action?" (subject)
  3. "WHAT receives the action?" (object)

Step 5: Guided Application (10 minutes)

Purpose: Pupils begin applying with teacher support

SAY: "You've done brilliantly identifying the parts! Now let's practice with your worksheets. I'll give you a few minutes to work on the first activity, then we'll share what you found."

DO:

  • Distribute appropriate worksheets (Support/Core/Challenge)
  • Direct pupils to Activity 1 on their worksheet
  • Circulate and provide support
  • Check for understanding with key pupils
  • Stop and share: ask 3-4 pupils to share their answers
  • Correct any misconceptions immediately

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch For:

  • Pupils confusing subject with object
  • Pupils selecting entire sentences instead of specific parts
  • Pupils struggling with "the" and "a" (include these with the noun)

Step 6: Independent PWP Work (15 minutes)

Purpose: Apply Subject + Verb + Object pattern in personal writing

SAY: "Now it's time for your PWP! You're going to write ONE sentence about YOUR story using our new formula: Subject + Verb + Object. Then you'll tell me if this sentence would be at the Beginning, Middle or End of your story. Let me show you an example first..."

DO:

  • Display slide 16-18 (PWP slides)
  • Model with your own example: "I visited my grandmother" ‚Üí Identify Subject/Verb/Object, then BME
  • Show ideas on slide 18 for subjects, verbs, objects
  • Direct pupils to PWP section of worksheet
  • Give 12 minutes independent work time
  • Circulate actively - this is heavy scaffolding phase
  • Provide sentence starters for struggling pupils
  • Check BME identification (Beginning/Middle/End)

üìù PWP Success Criteria

  • ‚úì Sentence includes a clear subject (who/what)
  • ‚úì Sentence includes an action verb
  • ‚úì Sentence includes an object (what receives action)
  • ‚úì Pupil can identify each part when asked
  • ‚úì Pupil identifies BME position correctly

Step 7: Share and Celebrate (5 minutes)

Purpose: Celebrate success and reinforce learning

SAY: "Let's hear some of your brilliant sentences! Who would like to share their PWP sentence? As you share, tell us your subject, verb and object, and whether it's Beginning, Middle or End of your story."

DO:

  • Select 4-6 pupils to share (aim for variety of examples)
  • Have class identify the Subject/Verb/Object in each
  • Praise specific successes: "I love how you chose an interesting verb!"
  • Display slide 19-20 (Remember! + Well Done!)
  • Preview next lesson: "Next time we'll add even more to our sentences!"
üîÑ Adaptation Guidance ‚ñº

Three-Tiered Approach

üü¢ SUPPORT Level (Extra Help)

When to Use: Pupils who struggle with identifying sentence parts, need visual supports, or are working below year level

Worksheet Features:

  • Word banks provided for all activities
  • Matching activities (instead of fill-in-blank)
  • Circling/underlining tasks (easier than writing)
  • Heavy PWP scaffolding (step-by-step)

Teaching Adaptations:

  • Work in small group or 1-1 during Step 6
  • Use sentence strips pupils can physically move
  • Provide extra modeling with more examples
  • Allow oral responses instead of written
  • Pre-highlight subjects in text before identifying verb/object

üí° Support Success Strategy

Create a reference card for each pupil:

  • Subject = WHO or WHAT (blue)
  • Verb = ACTION (red)
  • Object = RECEIVES (purple)

Include simple example: "The dog chased the ball"

üü° CORE Level (Standard)

When to Use: Most pupils working at expected year level

Worksheet Features:

  • Balanced activities (identify, label, apply)
  • True/False questions for checking understanding
  • Independent sentence writing with formula support
  • Medium PWP scaffolding (planning template)

Teaching Adaptations:

  • Standard whole-class instruction sufficient
  • Brief check-ins during independent work
  • Peer support partnerships
  • Self-check opportunities

üî¥ CHALLENGE Level (Extension)

When to Use: Pupils who quickly grasp concept and need deeper thinking

Worksheet Features:

  • Analysis table (analyze multiple sentences)
  • Error correction ("Spot the Mistake")
  • Multi-sentence paragraph writing
  • Minimal PWP scaffolding
  • Extra challenge (sentence transformation)

Teaching Adaptations:

  • Minimal teacher support during independent work
  • Challenge: "Can you write 3 sentences that all connect?"
  • Ask pupils to create their own examples for others
  • Introduce compound subjects/objects if appropriate

Year Group Considerations

Year Typical Level Adaptations
Year 2 Support or Core Use simpler vocabulary, more modeling, extended time, heavy visual supports
Year 3 Core Standard approach works well; some may need support, few need challenge
Year 4-5 Core or Challenge May grasp quickly; ready for challenge activities and extensions

SEN Adaptations

  • Working Memory: Reduce number of examples, use visual cards, allow note-taking
  • Processing Speed: Extra time, fewer activities, oral responses allowed
  • Fine Motor: Typing instead of handwriting, scribe support, verbal responses
  • Attention: Movement breaks, shorter segments, fidget tools, proximity seating
  • Language: Simplified vocabulary, visual supports, translation support if EAL

üîß Common Misconceptions & Solutions ‚ñº

Top 8 Misconceptions for Lesson 11

1. Confusing Subject with Object

What Pupils Say/Do: "The ball is the subject in 'The dog chased the ball'."

Why It Happens: Both are nouns; pupils pick the wrong one

Solution:

  • Always ask: "WHO or WHAT is DOING the action?" (subject)
  • Then: "WHAT is RECEIVING the action?" (object)
  • Use gestures: point to doer (subject), point away to receiver (object)
  • Practice with more obvious examples first: "I kicked the football" (I = doer)

2. Including Extra Words with the Verb

What Pupils Say/Do: Say the verb is "were eating" or "was rolling"

Why It Happens: Helping verbs confuse them

Solution:

  • For this lesson, accept "were eating" as the verb phrase (don't overcomplicate)
  • Focus on identifying the action part
  • Say: "Great! The action is 'were eating' - that's our verb."

3. Forgetting "The" or "A" with Subjects/Objects

What Pupils Say/Do: Write "cows" instead of "the cows" as subject

Why It Happens: Unsure if articles count

Solution:

  • Include articles with the noun: "the cows," "a pig," "my friend"
  • Explain: "We want the WHOLE subject, including 'the' or 'a'"
  • Model this consistently

4. Thinking Every Sentence Has an Object

What Pupils Say/Do: In "The dog barked," pupils try to find an object

Why It Happens: They've learned the pattern and think it's universal

Solution:

  • Acknowledge: "You're right, some sentences don't have objects!"
  • Say: "For today, we're focusing on sentences that DO have objects."
  • Don't worry about this too much at L11; it comes later

5. Confusing "Subject" with "Topic"

What Pupils Say/Do: "The subject is 'farm' because the story is about a farm"

Why It Happens: The word "subject" has multiple meanings

Solution:

  • Clarify: "The SUBJECT is WHO does the action in THIS sentence."
  • Avoid saying "what the sentence is about" (too vague)
  • Use "WHO or WHAT does the action" consistently

6. Identifying the Wrong Noun as Subject

What Pupils Say/Do: In "The farmer fed the chickens," say "chickens" is the subject

Why It Happens: Multiple nouns in sentence

Solution:

  • Start with the verb FIRST
  • Ask: "What's the action? Fed. WHO fed? The farmer!"
  • Then: "What did the farmer feed? The chickens (object)"
  • Order matters: Verb ‚Üí Subject ‚Üí Object questioning sequence

7. PWP Sentence Doesn't Follow Pattern

What Pupils Say/Do: Write "I went to the park" (no clear object)

Why It Happens: Pupils write what sounds natural vs. following formula

Solution:

  • Before writing, have pupils PLAN: Subject? Verb? Object?
  • Check plan before they write full sentence
  • Provide sentence frames if needed: "___ [verb] ___"
  • Give examples of good Subject + Verb + Object sentences

8. BME Confusion (Beginning/Middle/End)

What Pupils Say/Do: Mark every PWP sentence as "Beginning"

Why It Happens: Not thinking about story position

Solution:

  • Ask: "Where would this sentence go in YOUR story?"
  • Clarify: "Is this something that happens at the start? In the middle? At the end?"
  • Example: "I was born" = Beginning; "I went to school" = Middle; "I celebrated" = could be End
  • Remind: There's no one "right" answer, as long as reasoning is sound
